Crossing the Dolomites

Entry 112 Alps On foot South Tyrol, Italy · June 2026 · 14 min read

The Alta Via 1 begins in a bus queue. Braies is postcard-famous now, and the lake car park fills before the espresso machine at the kiosk has warmed up. But walk twenty minutes up the first switchbacks and the crowd thins to people carrying their own weight — the great sorting mechanism of every mountain range.

The Dolomites are not like other Alps. There is no gentle green introduction; the limestone stands straight up out of the meadows, pale as bone, and the trail threads the gaps. Geologists will tell you these were coral reefs once, which explains both the colour and the vertigo — you are, in a sense, walking the rim of a fossil lagoon two kilometres in the sky.

Every rifugio on the route serves polenta, and every one of them is certain the next hut over does it wrong.

Rifugio arithmetic

Hut-to-hut walking replaces navigation anxiety with dinner anxiety. Beds book out months ahead in high summer, so the route plans itself backwards from wherever said yes. Ours worked out to four stages, which the table below records the honest way — with the espresso stops counted.

What the table can't record is the light. At seven each evening the enrosadira arrives — the alpenglow the Ladin valleys named centuries ago — and the whole range goes rose-pink for a quarter of an hour. Conversation on the terraces stops. It does this every clear evening, and it is somehow never routine.

What I'd change

Nothing about the route; everything about the pack. The classic mistake survives every generation of gear: take the sleeping-bag liner, leave the second fleece. The huts are warm, the blankets are thick, and the climbs are long.
