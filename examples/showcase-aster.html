Issue 14 — Midsummer

The Cartography of Hedgerows

Before the surveyors came with their chains and theodolites, the parish was mapped in hawthorn and blackthorn. Marion Ashworth walks the oldest lines on the land.

By Marion Ashworth · 14 minute read

  1. Home
  2. Issue 14
  3. Essays

The oldest map of the parish is not in the county archive. It grows along the lane between the church and the disused mill: twelve feet of hawthorn, field maple, hazel and dog-rose, stitched through with bramble and briony, holding a line that was old before anyone thought to write it down. We are used to thinking of maps as things made of the land. A hedgerow is the reverse — a map made in the land, drawn in living wood, and still legible to anyone willing to slow to its pace.

There is even a method for dating one. In the 1960s the ecologist Max Hooper noticed that the number of woody species in a thirty-yard stretch of hedge corresponds, roughly, to its age in centuries. One species, one hundred years. A hedge of hawthorn alone is likely young — a creature of the modern survey. But stand at a stretch that carries hawthorn, blackthorn, hazel, holly, maple, spindle and wild service tree, and you are reading a seven-hundred-year-old sentence, still being revised.

You can tell the age of a hedge by its manners, too. The enclosure acts of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries ruled their boundaries straight, because they were drawn first on paper, in London, by men who had never stood in the fields they were dividing. The older lines wander. They follow a stream that has since sunk underground, skirt a boulder long ago carted off, bulge around the ghost of a tree. Where the parliamentary hedge is a statement, the assart hedge is a conversation — and the land always got the last word.

Reading the lines

To read a hedgerow, start at its feet. A bank with a ditch on one side records which field the hedge was planted to defend, and by whom; the ditch always lies on the neighbour's side, because you dug your boundary from your own soil. Look next for coppice stools — hazel cut and re-cut until its base has swollen into a fist of old wood, some of them older than the churches they can see. Then look up. A standard oak every chain or so was the planter's pension: timber for the grandchildren, shade for the stock, and a fixed point for every survey since.

A hedge is not a wall that happened to grow. It is a negotiation, renewed each winter, between what we plant and what escapes us.

The map nearly went the way of most old maps. Between the end of the war and the end of the century, England grubbed up roughly half its hedgerows — paid by the yard to do it — because a machine the width of a tennis court has no patience for a boundary drawn by a stream that no longer exists. What was lost was not only line but traffic: a hedge is a road for everything too small or too sensible to cross open ground, the dormouse's holloway, the yellowhammer's parish boundary, ten thousand miles of verge running from wood to wood.

From the archive

In Issue 3, Edith Calloway walked the same boundaries the other way — “Beating the Bounds”, on the parish perambulations that kept these lines in living memory before anyone kept them on paper.

And yet the lines are being redrawn. Every winter now, in cold fields, you can find people relearning the oldest of the boundary crafts: laying a hedge, cutting each stem almost through and bending it down along the line, so that the wound forces new growth from the base and the map thickens instead of thinning. It is slow work, and it looks, at first, like vandalism. But to lay a hedge is to write in the margin of a very old book — and to trust that someone, a century on, will stand in the lane, count seven species in thirty yards, and know that we were here, and that we kept the line.
